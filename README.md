# Medial-Axis-Transformation.io
Abstract— Classical methods for obtaining the medial axis of an object typically sacrifice topological integrity or produce wide multi-pixel skeletons that are sensitive to noise when used in practice. The Enhanced Grassfire Medial Axis Transform (EGF-MAT) offers a principled alternative by employing a wave propagation framework in which distance waves propagate simultaneously from obstacle boundaries and the border of the mapping region, assigning each free cell its nearest boundary distance. Skeletal candidates are then identified through the Common Vertices Meeting (CVM) technique as cells that are equally distant from two or more sources, after which a two-pass 64-pattern Look-Up Table thinning operator and a separate 64-pattern diagonal removal stage together produce a clean, fully connected, one-pixel-wide medial axis. Formal proofs of topological correctness cover all eight-connectivity configurations of the eight-neighborhood — a level of rigor not found in most existing skeletonization methods, classical or modern. Evaluated against 12 complex binary shapes and 13,797 images from the NIST Digits and MPEG-7 datasets, EGF-MAT achieved an overall quality score of 94.821%, a connectivity score of 97.8%, and strict one-pixel width across all skeletons, outperforming the Euclidean Distance Transform across all three criteria and producing significantly fewer spurious branches than Blum's classical method. Processing time scales approximately linearly with the number of cells — 1.04 ms for 1,000 cells and 7.16 ms for 10,000 cells — confirming that EGF-MAT provides an affordable and efficient means of skeletonizing objects for use in robotics, autonomous navigation, computer vision, and spatial analysis.

  Keywords:  Grassfire Algorithm, Wave Propagation, Medial Axis Transform, Skeletonization, Image Processing.

I. INTRODUCTION
T
HIS  skeletonization techniques originate from Blum's medial axis transform (MAT) [1]. While they offer geometric robustness, they do require some attention to the specific application domain when deployed (for instance, noise concerns and real-time performance) [2]. Recent Advances in wavefront-driven skeletonization have concentrated on enhancing efficiency, notably via parallelization and the integration of skeletonization algorithms with heuristic search methods [3]. Many researchers and practitioners still utilize wavefront methods, such as grassfire, for real-time planning purposes. One specific implementation of wavefront methods is like that used in geometric combustion. The grassfire algorithm propagates a distance field from , obstacles to define how far away a path is from an obstacle, and it distills the relevant information in the form of a collision-free corridor [4]. Voronoi diagrams further bolster this by segmenting space in a way that mimics what we find in skeletal structures. Nearly every application that allows for the digital extraction of skeletons will take advantage of some variation on thinning algorithms that successively erode what is viewed as the boundary of an object until only a one-pixel or voxel-wide representation remains that preserves connectivity [5]. Thinning that is accurate will not incur topological errors (such as isolated regions or cavitations that have been merged), as thinning does not occur in shells and can appropriately propagate [6]. Thinning can be improved upon if the object may be termed a non-deformable solid — that is, a medium that does not reshape in a manner requiring object evolution — using wave propagation models that simulate how fire propagates through the interior of such objects [7]. The skeleton will be minimal, symmetric with its mass centroid, can be used for path planning without complication, and although it may produce potential connections that may unnecessarily confuse roboticists and spatial scientists alike, it is streamlined to be most appropriate for roboticists and spatial scientists rethinking navigation for their objectives [8, 9]. Thinning essentially represents an insightful geometric idea that enables computational efficiency in taking skeletal extraction, potentially in useful ways confined under real-world constraints [10]. Unfortunately, current algorithms based on two-dimensional geometries remain hampered by irregular geometries and dynamic obstacle updates, limiting real-world deployment in tasks such as UAV navigation in three-dimensional environments and multi-agent systems [11]. Skeletonization of environmental spaces is a common way of converting a free space into the intersection of a network of medial axes, so that it can be closely examined while still preserving connectivity in the planning space [12]. Skeletonizing free space can be especially effective with wave propagation techniques or sub-methods such as grassfire algorithms, where wave propagation is used to simulate the collision of wavefronts, resulting in a first collision location that serves as a skeletal point [13]. Path planning will always be essential to any autonomous navigation scenario, allowing robots and agents to effectively plan for safe navigation in a complex environment [14]. The Medial Axis Transformation (MAT) is a key idea in the analysis of shapes and the representation of objects. It provides a compact skeleton of the shape while preserving the geometric and topological properties of the structure in question. Figure 1 demonstrates that the Classical or Theoretical MAT was described by Blum’s Grassfire Transform [1], which defines the medial axis as the set of points at which fire fronts, propagating inward from the object’s boundary, converge. This approach is elegant and geometrically well-defined. Lee [15] later formalized this in an algorithmic form for planar shapes, and then [16]  provided the mathematical foundations of the medial axis transform by defining it as the locus of centers of maximal inscribed circles (and spheres) which simultaneously touch the boundary in at least two positions.
